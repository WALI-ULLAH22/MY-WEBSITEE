Viral Hepatitis (A, B, C)

Viral hepatitis includes several distinct infections (A, B, C, D, E) that cause liver inflammation. Hepatitis A usually causes acute illness, while hepatitis B and C can become chronic and lead to cirrhosis or liver cancer. Vaccines are available for hepatitis A and B; hepatitis C is now often curable with direct-acting antivirals.

  • Modes of spread: Hep A — fecal-oral; Hep B & C — blood and body fluids
  • Vaccines: Effective vaccines for Hep A and Hep B
  • Treatment: Hep C curable with DAAs; Hep B manageable with antivirals

Transmission & Symptoms

Hepatitis A spreads via contaminated food or water. Hepatitis B and C spread through exposure to infectious blood or body fluids. Symptoms vary from mild (fatigue, nausea, jaundice) to severe liver dysfunction.

Diagnosis & Treatment

Serologic and molecular tests identify the specific hepatitis virus. Hepatitis A typically resolves on its own; supportive care is used. Hepatitis B may require long-term antiviral therapy to suppress replication. Hepatitis C is treated with direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) that cure most infections.

Prevention

Vaccination against hepatitis A and B is highly effective. Other measures include safe injection and blood-screening practices, harm reduction for people who inject drugs, food/water hygiene, and testing programs to identify and treat infections early.

Global Statistics & Impact

Chronic Infections: Around 354 million people worldwide

Hepatitis B and C cause long-term liver infections and account for most chronic cases, creating a significant global health burden.
