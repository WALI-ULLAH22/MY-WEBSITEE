Dengue Virus

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral disease transmitted mainly by the Aedes aegypti mosquito. It is common in tropical and subtropical regions, especially in urban areas with poor sanitation and standing water where mosquitoes can breed.

Symptoms usually appear 4–10 days after infection and include high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, nausea, vomiting, skin rash, and extreme weakness. Dengue is often called “breakbone fever” because of the intense body pain it causes.

In some cases, dengue can develop into severe dengue, also known as dengue hemorrhagic fever. This serious form can cause internal bleeding, a drop in blood pressure, organ failure, and death if not treated quickly. There is no specific antiviral medicine for dengue, so treatment focuses on pain relief, hydration, and close medical monitoring.

Transmission

Dengue virus is transmitted to humans through the bite of infected Aedes mosquitoes (Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus). The virus does not spread directly from person to person except in rare circumstances (e.g., blood transfusion).

Symptoms

Symptoms typically appear 4–10 days after infection and include high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, nausea, vomiting, skin rash, and extreme weakness. Severe dengue can cause bleeding, plasma leakage, shock, and organ impairment.

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis is by serologic tests (IgM/IgG) or molecular tests (RT-PCR) early in illness. There is no specific antiviral therapy; treatment is supportive and focuses on careful fluid management, pain control (avoid NSAIDs like ibuprofen and aspirin), and close monitoring for warning signs of severe disease.

Prevention

Prevention depends largely on mosquito control: remove standing water, apply insect repellent, install window screens, use bed nets where appropriate, and implement community vector-control programs. Vaccination is available in select settings and for specific age/serostatus groups.

LATEST UPDATE

Some new dengue vaccines have been licensed or authorized in specific countries; public-health use is guided by local epidemiology and prior exposure considerations.

Global Statistics & Impact

Estimated Cases: Approximately 390 million infections per year

Dengue mainly affects tropical and subtropical regions. Rapid urbanization and climate change have contributed to the rise in cases over recent decades.
