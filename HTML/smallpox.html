Smallpox

Smallpox, caused by the variola virus, was a severe and often fatal infectious disease. A global vaccination campaign led to its eradication in 1980 — one of public health’s greatest achievements.

  • Agent: Variola virus (Orthopoxvirus)
  • Transmission: Prolonged face-to-face contact, respiratory droplets, direct contact with lesions or contaminated materials
  • Outcome historically: High morbidity and ~30% case-fatality in severe forms
  • Status: Eradicated (last naturally occurring case 1977; declared eradicated 1980)

Transmission

Historically, smallpox spread primarily through prolonged face-to-face contact and respiratory droplets. Direct contact with infectious material from skin lesions or contaminated objects (bedding, clothing) could also transmit the virus. Transmission required relatively close and sustained exposure compared with many modern respiratory viruses.

Signs & Symptoms

Smallpox began with fever, severe malaise, headache and body aches. Within a few days a characteristic rash appeared that progressed from macules to papules to fluid-filled pustules and then scabs. Lesions were typically synchronous (at the same stage) and commonly left deep, pitted scars. Severe cases could cause blindness and death.

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis in the pre-eradication era relied on the clinical presentation and laboratory testing of lesion samples (PCR, viral culture). There is no widely proven antiviral therapy that was used routinely for smallpox in large populations; supportive care was the mainstay. In recent decades, antiviral agents (developed for orthopoxviruses) are retained for emergency use and research.

Prevention & Vaccination

The key to smallpox elimination was vaccination using the vaccinia virus (the smallpox vaccine). Mass vaccination campaigns and surveillance-containment strategies (ring vaccination) interrupted transmission and led to eradication. Routine vaccination of the general public ceased after eradication; limited vaccination continues for laboratory workers and select biodefense personnel.

History & Global Eradication

Smallpox was responsible for huge mortality and suffering for centuries. A coordinated global eradication campaign led by the World Health Organization during the 1960s–1970s combined mass vaccination, surveillance and targeted containment. The last naturally occurring case was recorded in 1977; the World Health Assembly declared smallpox eradicated in 1980.

Bioterrorism Preparedness

Although smallpox no longer occurs naturally, the variola virus is retained in secure laboratories. Public health agencies maintain preparedness plans (vaccines, diagnostics, response protocols) to address the theoretical risk of deliberate release. Vaccination and rapid identification would be central to any outbreak response.

Latest Update

Smallpox remains eradicated worldwide. Vaccination is not recommended for the general public. Surveillance, secure storage policies for virus stocks and preparedness measures remain in place internationally.

Global Statistics & Impact

Historical Cases: 300–500 million cases in the 20th century

Smallpox was one of the deadliest viruses in human history before its eradication. Through global vaccination efforts, it became the first disease officially eliminated worldwide in 1980.
