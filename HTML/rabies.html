Rabies

Rabies is a vaccine-preventable viral disease that affects the central nervous system. It is nearly 100% fatal once clinical symptoms develop, but prompt post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) is highly effective at preventing illness.

  • Agent: Rabies virus (Lyssavirus)
  • Most common source: Dog bites (~99% of human cases)
  • Prevention: Dog vaccination, wound care, PEP
  • Global status: Endemic in many low- and middle-income countries; elimination goals aim to end dog-mediated human deaths by 2030

Transmission

Rabies is transmitted when infectious saliva or neural tissue from an infected animal enters a wound or mucous membrane, most commonly through bites. Dogs are the principal source globally; in some regions bats and wildlife are important reservoirs.

Symptoms

Early symptoms are nonspecific (fever, headache, malaise). As disease progresses, neurological signs appear: agitation, confusion, hydrophobia, excess salivation, paralysis and coma. Once symptoms appear, rabies is almost always fatal.

Diagnosis & Treatment

Laboratory tests (PCR, antigen detection) can confirm infection from saliva, skin, or brain tissue. There is no reliably effective treatment after symptom onset. The critical intervention is prompt post-exposure prophylaxis: thorough wound washing, administration of rabies vaccine doses, and rabies immunoglobulin when indicated.

Prevention

Preventive measures include mass dog vaccination, responsible pet ownership, public education on bite prevention, pre-exposure vaccination for high-risk people, and immediate access to PEP for exposed individuals. WHO supports strategies to improve vaccine access and cost-effective intradermal dosing where appropriate.

Latest Update

Global initiatives such as WHO’s “Zero by 30” aim to end human deaths from dog-mediated rabies by 2030 through combined vaccination, surveillance and access to PEP.

Global Statistics & Impact

Annual Impact: About 59,000 human deaths each year

Rabies cases are relatively low compared to other viruses, but the disease is almost always fatal once symptoms appear. Most cases occur due to dog bites in developing regions.
