Safety Guidance for Viral Diseases

Practical safety advice for individuals, households and communities when facing viral infections. Includes immediate steps after exposure, caring for sick people, and reducing risk for others.

General Safety Principles

  • Avoid close contact with people who are sick and stay home if you feel unwell.
  • Maintain good hand hygiene: wash hands with soap and water for 20 seconds or use alcohol-based hand rub.
  • Cover coughs and sneezes with a tissue or the inside of your elbow; dispose tissues safely.
  • Keep shared surfaces clean and well ventilated indoor spaces to reduce airborne risk.
  • Follow local public health guidance about testing, isolation, and mask use during outbreaks.

After Suspected Exposure

If you think you were exposed to a virus: monitor symptoms, limit contact with others (especially high-risk people), get tested when appropriate, and follow public health instructions about quarantine or post-exposure prophylaxis where recommended (e.g., rabies PEP, hepatitis B immunoglobulin in special cases).

Caring for Someone Ill

  • Designate one care-giver when possible and use basic protective measures (mask, hand hygiene).
  • Keep the sick person in a well-ventilated single room if available, and minimize shared items.
  • Seek immediate medical care for warning signs (difficulty breathing, persistent chest pain, altered mental state, high fever not responding to treatment).
  • Follow healthcare instructions for isolation duration and return-to-work/school criteria.

Virus-specific Safety Notes

COVID-19

Isolate when symptomatic or testing positive. Wear a well-fitting mask around others for recommended duration. Test and follow local guidance for return to normal activities.

Influenza

Stay home during fever and until 24 hours after fever resolves without medication. Antiviral drugs may shorten illness if started early in high-risk patients.

Dengue & Zika

Avoid mosquito bites if symptomatic; use bed nets and repellents. Pregnant people should seek medical advice after possible Zika exposure.

Rabies

Any bite from a potentially rabid animal requires urgent wound cleaning and immediate medical evaluation for post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP).

HIV

For occupational or sexual exposure, get urgent medical evaluation; post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) can prevent infection if started promptly.

Hepatitis

Follow blood-safety precautions; seek evaluation for possible post-exposure vaccination or immunoglobulin when recommended.

Ebola

Ebola requires strict isolation and specialized clinical care; follow public health instructions for contacts and containment.

Yellow Fever & Smallpox

Yellow fever: seek care for severe illness and ensure vaccination for travel to endemic regions. Smallpox is eradicated but lab-safety protocols apply where virus stocks exist.
